# Credit Risk – Complete Beginner to Professional Guide

---

## Importance of Credit Risk Knowledge

Credit risk is one of the most important risks in banking and finance. Banks lend money to individuals and businesses every day. However, there is always a possibility that the borrower may fail to repay the loan.

Understanding credit risk helps financial institutions:

* Protect their capital
* Maintain financial stability
* Reduce losses from loan defaults
* Improve decision-making when lending

Without proper credit risk knowledge, banks may lend money to borrowers who are unable to repay, which can lead to major financial losses.

---

# Definition of Credit Risk

Credit Risk is the risk that a borrower will fail to repay a loan according to the agreed terms.

In simple words:

> Credit Risk = Risk that the borrower will not pay back the money.

### Example

A bank lends ₹1,00,000 to a customer.

If the customer:

* Pays the loan on time → No credit risk realized
* Fails to repay → Bank faces credit risk loss

---

# Who is a Borrower?

A **borrower** is any individual, business, or government entity that takes money from a lender with a promise to repay it in the future.

### Types of Borrowers

| Borrower Type | Example                  |
| ------------- | ------------------------ |
| Individual    | Personal loan, home loan |
| Business      | Business expansion loan  |
| Government    | Government bonds         |

### Example

* A person taking a home loan from a bank
* A company borrowing money for expansion

Both are borrowers.

---

# Concept of Risk in Banking

Risk means **uncertainty of loss**.

In banking, risks arise when banks perform activities such as:

* Lending money
* Investing in securities
* Providing financial guarantees

Whenever a bank lends money, there is always a chance that the borrower may default.

---

# Combining Credit and Risk

When we combine **credit (lending money)** and **risk (possibility of loss)**, we get:

> **Credit Risk**

This means the **risk of financial loss due to a borrower failing to repay the loan.**

---

# Historical Background & Regulatory Evolution

Financial systems evolved significantly after major crises, especially the global financial crisis of 2008.

Regulators around the world introduced stricter rules to make the banking system safer.

---

# 2008 Financial Crisis

The **2008 financial crisis** was one of the worst financial disasters in modern history.

### What Happened

Banks in the United States gave large numbers of **subprime mortgages** (loans to risky borrowers).

Many borrowers could not repay their loans.

This caused:

* Massive loan defaults
* Collapse of mortgage-backed securities
* Huge losses for banks and financial institutions

### Result

Major financial institutions collapsed or required government bailouts.

---

# Regulatory Measures Post-Crisis

After the crisis, global regulators introduced stronger rules to prevent another systemic collapse.

Key objectives included:

* Increasing bank capital requirements
* Strengthening risk management systems
* Improving transparency
* Enhancing stress testing

These measures were mainly introduced through **Basel regulations**.

---

# How Banks Operate: Demand, Supply & Profit

Banks play a critical role in the economy by connecting people who have money with people who need money.

---

# Banks in the Economy

Banks act as **financial intermediaries**.

They collect deposits from people who want to save money and lend that money to borrowers who need funds.

### Two Sides of Banking

| Side             | Description                      |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Supply of Funds  | Depositors saving money in banks |
| Demand for Funds | Borrowers needing loans          |

---

# Fund Channeling

Banks move money from **savers to borrowers**.

Example flow:

Depositor → Bank → Borrower

Example:

1. People deposit money into savings accounts.
2. The bank collects these deposits.
3. The bank lends that money as home loans, car loans, or business loans.

---

# Profit Generation

Banks earn profit mainly through **interest spread**.

### Interest Spread

Interest Spread = Loan Interest − Deposit Interest

Example:

| Activity         | Interest Rate |
| ---------------- | ------------- |
| Deposit Interest | 4%            |
| Loan Interest    | 10%           |

Profit margin = **6% spread**

Banks must carefully balance **profit and risk** while lending.

---

# Risk Mitigation Techniques in Practice

Banks implement multiple strategies to reduce the probability of losses.

---

# Bank Profitability & Risk

Banks prefer **low-risk borrowers** because they are more likely to repay loans.

### Example

| Borrower            | Risk Level |
| ------------------- | ---------- |
| Salaried individual | Low risk   |
| New startup         | High risk  |

Banks often prefer lending to stable borrowers like households.

---

# Economic Agents in Lending

Credit markets involve multiple participants.

| Economic Agent         | Role                          |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Households             | Borrow or deposit money       |
| Banks                  | Provide loans                 |
| Producers / Businesses | Borrow capital for production |

Funds circulate between these agents to support economic activity.

---

# The 5Cs of Credit Risk

Banks use the **5Cs framework** to evaluate borrowers.

| C          | Meaning              | Description                                        |
| ---------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Character  | Trustworthiness      | Borrower's credit history and reputation           |
| Capacity   | Repayment ability    | Ability to generate income and repay loans         |
| Collateral | Security             | Assets pledged against the loan                    |
| Conditions | Economic environment | Market and economic conditions affecting repayment |
| Capital    | Financial strength   | Borrower’s own investment or net worth             |

### Example

A borrower applying for a business loan will be evaluated based on:

* Their credit score
* Income and cash flow
* Assets pledged as collateral
* Economic conditions of the industry
* Personal financial investment in the business

# Credit Risk Governance: Policies & Standards

Banks use strict internal policies to manage credit risk.

---

# Robust Credit Policies

Credit policies define **rules for lending decisions**.

These policies help banks:

* Maintain consistency in loan approvals
* Avoid risky borrowers
* Ensure regulatory compliance

---

# Underwriting Standards

Underwriting is the process of **evaluating a borrower before approving a loan**.

It involves detailed financial analysis of the borrower.

---

# Purpose of Underwriting

The primary objective of underwriting is to ensure that:

> Only creditworthy borrowers receive loans.

This reduces the probability of loan defaults.

---

# Loan Assessment Criteria

Loan approvals are often guided by regulatory authorities.

Examples:

* RBI (Reserve Bank of India)
* FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation)

These regulators provide guidelines for safe lending practices.

---

# Allowance, Monitoring & Credit Risk Management

Banks continuously monitor loan portfolios and maintain provisions for losses.

---

# Loan Loss Allowance

Banks maintain **loan loss provisions** to cover expected loan defaults.

This reserve is often referred to as **Allowance for Loan Losses (ALLL)**.

Example:

If a bank expects ₹10 crore in potential loan losses, it will set aside that amount as a provision.

---

# Importance of Capital Reserves

Capital reserves act as a financial cushion.

They ensure that banks can absorb losses during financial stress.

These reserves should not be used to generate profits.

---

# Ongoing Credit Risk Monitoring

Credit risk management does not end after loan approval.

Banks continuously monitor borrowers to detect early signs of financial distress.

---

# Key Monitoring Factors

Common warning indicators include:

* Falling credit scores
* Declining business performance
* Legal disputes or lawsuits
* Missed payments

Early detection helps banks take preventive action.

---

# Advanced Risk Management Tools

Modern banks use advanced analytical techniques to manage risk.

---

# Stress Testing

Stress testing evaluates how banks would perform under extreme economic conditions.

Example scenarios:

* Economic recession
* Housing market crash
* Large-scale unemployment

Stress tests help regulators ensure banks remain financially stable.

---

# Capital Adequacy Requirements (CAR)

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) measures a bank's ability to absorb losses.

Formula:

```
CAR = (Bank Capital / Risk Weighted Assets) × 100
```

Higher CAR indicates a stronger and more stable bank.

# Additional Risk Mitigation Methods

Banks use several additional techniques to reduce credit risk exposure.

---

# Diversification Strategy

Diversification reduces concentration risk.

Example:

Instead of lending heavily to one industry, banks distribute loans across:

* Manufacturing
* Technology
* Real estate
* Retail

This prevents large losses if one sector collapses.

---

# Credit Insurance & Collateral

Banks reduce risk by securing loans with:

* Collateral assets
* Credit insurance

If a borrower defaults, the bank can recover losses through these protections.

---

# Efficient Collection Practices

Collection teams follow structured procedures to recover delinquent loans.

These reports help banks evaluate borrower reliability.

# Bonus: Deep Dive into 2008 Crisis

The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated the consequences of weak risk management.

---

# Overview of 2008 Financial Crisis

The crisis began in the United States housing market.

Banks issued high-risk mortgage loans that were later bundled into financial products and sold globally.

When housing prices fell, borrowers defaulted in large numbers.

---

# Market Collapse Effects

The collapse of mortgage-backed securities caused global financial markets to crash.

Stock markets fell sharply and liquidity dried up.

---

# Institutional Impact

Many major financial institutions suffered severe losses.

Some banks failed completely while others required government rescue packages.

---

# Mergers & Acquisitions

During the crisis, many banks merged or were acquired by stronger institutions to prevent financial collapse.

These emergency mergers helped stabilize the global financial system.
