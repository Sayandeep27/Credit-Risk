# Loss Given Default (LGD) — Complete Guide

---

## 1. Introduction: Why LGD Matters for Banks and Financial Systems

Loss Given Default (LGD) is one of the most important concepts in **credit risk management**. Banks lend money to individuals and businesses, but not all borrowers repay their loans. When a borrower fails to repay, the bank experiences a loss.

LGD measures **how much money the bank actually loses after a borrower defaults**.

Understanding LGD helps banks:

* Estimate potential financial losses
* Price loans correctly
* Maintain financial stability
* Comply with regulatory requirements

If banks fail to estimate LGD properly, they may underestimate risk, which can threaten the stability of the financial system.

---

## 2. What is LGD? (Simple Definition)

**Loss Given Default (LGD)** is the percentage of the total loan exposure that a lender loses when a borrower defaults.

In simple words:

> LGD tells us how much of the loan the bank cannot recover after default.

### LGD Formula

```
LGD = (Loss after default / Total exposure at default)
```

or

```
LGD = 1 − Recovery Rate
```

Where:

* **Recovery Rate** = Portion of loan recovered through payments, collateral, or legal action.

### Simple Example

| Item             | Amount |
| ---------------- | ------ |
| Loan Given       | ₹120   |
| Amount Recovered | ₹45    |
| Loss             | ₹75    |

```
LGD = 75 / 120 = 0.625 = 62.5%
```

This means the bank lost **62.5% of the loan**.

---

## 3. The Core of Credit Risk: PD, LGD, and EAD

In banking, credit risk is mainly measured using **three key components**.

| Component                       | Meaning                                   | What It Measures  |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| **PD (Probability of Default)** | Likelihood that the borrower will default | Chance of default |
| **LGD (Loss Given Default)**    | Percentage of loss if default occurs      | Severity of loss  |
| **EAD (Exposure at Default)**   | Total outstanding amount at default       | Amount at risk    |

### Relationship Between Them

These three variables are used to calculate **Expected Credit Loss (ECL)**.

```
ECL = PD × LGD × EAD
```

### Example

| Variable | Value    |
| -------- | -------- |
| PD       | 10%      |
| LGD      | 50%      |
| EAD      | ₹100,000 |

```
ECL = 0.10 × 0.50 × 100000
ECL = ₹5,000
```

This means the **expected loss for the bank is ₹5,000**.

---

## 4. LGD Calculation Example (Step-by-Step)

Suppose a bank lends **₹120** to a borrower.

The borrower defaults and the bank recovers **₹45** through collections or collateral.

### Step 1 — Calculate Loss

```
Loss = Loan Amount − Recovery
Loss = 120 − 45 = 75
```

### Step 2 — Calculate LGD

```
LGD = Loss / Exposure
LGD = 75 / 120
LGD = 0.625
```

### Step 3 — Convert to Percentage

```
LGD = 62.5%
```

This means the bank **lost 62.5% of the loan value**.

---

## 5. When Is a Borrower Considered in Default?

According to most global banking regulations (Basel guidelines and many regulators), a borrower is typically considered in **default** when:

```
The payment is overdue for more than 90 days
```

This is commonly called the **90‑Day Past Due Rule (DPD 90)**.

### Example

| Loan EMI Due | Status                     |
| ------------ | -------------------------- |
| Day 1–89     | Delinquent but not default |
| Day 90+      | Considered Default         |

Once the borrower crosses **90 days past due**, banks classify the account as a **defaulted loan** and begin loss estimation and recovery processes.

## 6. Realized LGD vs Override LGD

Banks often estimate LGD using historical data. However, sometimes adjustments are needed.

### Realized LGD

Realized LGD is the **actual loss experienced after the recovery process is completed**.

It is calculated using real recovery cash flows.

Example:

| Loan         | ₹100 |
| ------------ | ---- |
| Recovered    | ₹40  |
| Realized LGD | 60%  |

### Override LGD

Override LGD occurs when **risk experts adjust the model output** based on additional information.

Reasons for override:

* Market conditions
* Collateral value changes
* Legal recovery challenges
* Economic downturn

Example:

Model LGD = 40%

Risk team overrides LGD to **55%** due to declining collateral value.

## 7. Step‑by‑Step LGD Computation in Banks

Banks follow structured steps to estimate LGD.

### Step 1 — Identify Defaulted Loans

Accounts that meet the **default definition (e.g., 90 DPD)**.

### Step 2 — Track Recoveries

Banks track recovery sources such as:

* Customer payments
* Collateral liquidation
* Legal recoveries

### Step 3 — Calculate Net Loss

```
Net Loss = Exposure − Total Recovery
```

### Step 4 — Compute LGD

```
LGD = Net Loss / Exposure
```

### Step 5 — Build LGD Models

Banks build statistical models using:

* historical recovery data
* collateral type
* loan characteristics
* economic conditions

These models estimate **future LGD values**.

---

## 8. Accounting Method vs Cash Flow Method

Banks typically estimate LGD using two major approaches.

### 1. Accounting LGD

This method uses accounting records to estimate losses.

```
LGD = (Write-offs − Recoveries) / Exposure
```

Characteristics:

* Simpler method
* Uses accounting data
* Less precise

### 2. Cash Flow LGD

This method tracks **actual cash recoveries over time** and discounts them to present value.

```
LGD = (Exposure − Present Value of Recoveries) / Exposure
```

Characteristics:

* More accurate
* Used in advanced risk models
* Required under many regulatory frameworks

## 9. Real-World Applications of LGD

Banks use LGD in many critical decisions.

### Loan Pricing

Higher LGD → higher interest rates.

### Capital Requirement

Banks must hold capital proportional to risk.

### Portfolio Risk Management

LGD helps identify **high-loss loan segments**.

### Stress Testing

Banks simulate crisis scenarios to estimate losses.

Accurate LGD models help banks **avoid billions of dollars in losses**.

## 10. Role of Regulators (RBI, OSFI)

Financial regulators ensure banks manage credit risk properly.

Examples include:

* Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
* Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)

Regulators require banks to:

* Maintain minimum capital
* Estimate PD, LGD, and EAD properly
* Follow global standards like Basel

This ensures that banks remain **financially stable and resilient during economic downturns**.

Banks assign **risk ratings** to borrowers based on their creditworthiness.

These ratings help estimate PD and LGD.

| AA     | Low Risk       |

## 12. Customer Risk Ratings (CRR) Simplified

Customer Risk Rating (CRR) is an internal scoring system used by banks to evaluate borrower risk.

CRR considers multiple factors:

| Factor           | Example              |
| ---------------- | -------------------- |
| Income stability | Salary consistency   |
| Credit history   | Past loan repayment  |
| Debt level       | Existing liabilities |
| Industry risk    | Business sector risk |

Example:

A borrower with:

* stable job
* good credit history
* low debt

will receive a **better CRR rating**.

Better CRR → lower PD → lower expected losses.

## 13. EAD and PD as Transactional Risk Factors

In credit risk modeling, **PD and EAD vary across transactions**.

### Exposure at Default (EAD)

EAD represents the **amount outstanding at the time of default**.

Example:

A borrower has a credit card limit of **₹100,000** but has used **₹70,000** when default occurs.

```
EAD = ₹70,000
```

### Probability of Default (PD)

PD estimates the **likelihood that the borrower will default within a specific time horizon**.

Example:

If PD = **5%**, then the borrower has a **5% chance of defaulting within the next year**.

These factors combine with LGD to estimate **expected loss**.

## 14. Final Takeaways on LGD and Industry Practices

Key points to remember:

* **LGD measures the percentage of loss after default**.
* It is a critical component of **credit risk modeling**.
* Banks combine **PD, LGD, and EAD** to estimate expected losses.
* Accurate LGD estimation improves **risk management and financial stability**.
* Regulators require banks to maintain robust LGD models.

In modern banking systems, LGD models help institutions **predict losses, price loans properly, and maintain adequate capital buffers**.
