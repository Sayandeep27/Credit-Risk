# Capital Adequacy in Credit Risk

---

## 1. What is Capital Adequacy?

Capital adequacy refers to the amount of capital a bank must maintain to absorb potential losses and protect depositors.

Banks lend money to individuals and companies. If borrowers fail to repay their loans, the bank faces **credit risk**. To ensure banks remain stable even during losses, regulators require banks to hold a minimum level of capital.

This requirement is known as **Capital Adequacy**.

In simple terms:

> Capital Adequacy = Safety cushion for banks

It ensures that even if some loans default, the bank will not collapse.

---

## 2. Why Capital Adequacy is Important

Capital adequacy protects:

1. Depositors' money
2. Financial system stability
3. Bank solvency during economic crises

Example:

If a bank gives loans worth $100 million and $5 million becomes bad loans, the bank must have enough capital to absorb that loss.

Without capital adequacy rules, banks could collapse during financial stress.

---

## 3. Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)

The **Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)** measures how much capital a bank has relative to its risk exposure.

### Formula

```
CAR = (Bank Capital / Risk Weighted Assets) × 100
```

Where:

* **Bank Capital** = money available to absorb losses
* **Risk Weighted Assets (RWA)** = assets adjusted for risk level

---

## 4. Types of Bank Capital

Banks maintain two major types of capital.

| Capital Type   | Description                                       |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Tier 1 Capital | Core capital such as equity and retained earnings |
| Tier 2 Capital | Supplementary capital like subordinated debt      |

### Tier 1 Capital

This is the **strongest and most reliable capital**.

Examples:

* Common equity
* Retained earnings

It can immediately absorb losses.

### Tier 2 Capital

This is **additional capital used as backup**.

Examples:

* Subordinated debt
* Hybrid capital instruments

---

## 5. Risk Weighted Assets (RWA)

Not all assets have the same risk.

Example:

| Asset            | Risk Level  |
| ---------------- | ----------- |
| Government bonds | Low risk    |
| Home loans       | Medium risk |
| Corporate loans  | High risk   |

Banks assign **risk weights** to assets.

Example:

| Asset           | Amount | Risk Weight | RWA |
| --------------- | ------ | ----------- | --- |
| Government bond | 100    | 0%          | 0   |
| Home loan       | 100    | 50%         | 50  |
| Corporate loan  | 100    | 100%        | 100 |

Total RWA = 150

---

## 6. Example of CAR Calculation

Suppose:

Bank Capital = $15 million
Risk Weighted Assets = $120 million

```
CAR = (15 / 120) × 100
CAR = 12.5%
```

If regulators require **minimum CAR of 10%**, the bank is considered safe.

---

## 7. Basel Framework Overview

The **Basel Committee on Banking Supervision** created global banking regulations to control risk.

These regulations are called the **Basel Accords**.

There are three main frameworks:

| Basel Framework | Year | Main Focus                           |
| --------------- | ---- | ------------------------------------ |
| Basel I         | 1988 | Minimum capital requirement          |
| Basel II        | 2004 | Risk-sensitive capital measurement   |
| Basel III       | 2010 | Stronger capital and liquidity rules |

---

## 8. Basel I

Basel I introduced a simple rule:

```
Minimum CAR = 8%
```

Assets were grouped into risk categories.

Example risk weights:

| Asset Type            | Risk Weight |
| --------------------- | ----------- |
| Government securities | 0%          |
| Mortgage loans        | 50%         |
| Corporate loans       | 100%        |

Limitation:

It was **too simple** and did not capture real risk accurately.

---

## 9. Basel II

Basel II introduced a **three pillar structure**.

### Pillar 1 — Minimum Capital Requirement

Covers three risks:

1. Credit Risk
2. Market Risk
3. Operational Risk

### Pillar 2 — Supervisory Review

Regulators review bank risk management practices.

### Pillar 3 — Market Discipline

Banks must disclose risk information publicly.

Basel II also introduced **advanced credit risk models**.

---

## 10. Basel III

Basel III was introduced after the **2008 financial crisis**.

Main improvements:

* Higher capital requirements
* Better quality capital
* Liquidity requirements
* Capital buffers

Minimum requirements include:

| Requirement | Minimum |
| ----------- | ------- |
| CET1        | 4.5%    |
| Tier 1      | 6%      |
| Total CAR   | 8%      |

Plus additional **capital conservation buffer**.

---

## 11. Credit Risk Measurement in Basel

Credit risk models estimate how likely a borrower will default and how much loss the bank may suffer.

Key components:

1. PD
2. LGD
3. EAD

These are used to estimate **Expected Loss (EL)**.

---

## 12. Probability of Default (PD)

**PD** measures the probability that a borrower will default within a certain time period.

Example:

If PD = 2%

This means:

Out of 100 similar borrowers, about **2 may default**.

Example table:

| Borrower   | Loan    | PD |
| ---------- | ------- | -- |
| Customer A | $10,000 | 1% |
| Customer B | $10,000 | 5% |

Customer B is riskier.

---

## 13. Loss Given Default (LGD)

**LGD** measures the percentage of loss if the borrower defaults.

Formula:

```
LGD = Loss / Exposure
```

Example:

Loan = $100,000
Recovered after default = $40,000

Loss = $60,000

```
LGD = 60,000 / 100,000
LGD = 60%
```

---

## 14. Exposure at Default (EAD)

**EAD** represents the total amount outstanding when the borrower defaults.

Example:

Loan limit = $50,000
Borrower has used = $35,000

If default occurs:

```
EAD = $35,000
```

For credit cards or revolving loans, EAD may include **future drawdowns**.

---

## 15. Expected Loss (EL)

Banks combine PD, LGD, and EAD to estimate expected loss.

### Formula

```
Expected Loss = PD × LGD × EAD
```

---

## 16. Expected Loss Example

Suppose:

PD = 5%
LGD = 60%
EAD = $100,000

```
EL = 0.05 × 0.60 × 100,000
EL = $3,000
```

This means the bank expects an **average loss of $3,000** from this loan.

---

## 17. How These Concepts Work Together

| Concept | Meaning                            |
| ------- | ---------------------------------- |
| PD      | Probability borrower defaults      |
| LGD     | Percentage loss if default happens |
| EAD     | Amount exposed at default          |
| EL      | Expected average loss              |
| CAR     | Bank capital protection ratio      |

These metrics help banks:

* measure credit risk
* calculate capital requirements
* remain financially stable

---

## 18. Simple End‑to‑End Example

A bank gives a corporate loan.

| Parameter | Value      |
| --------- | ---------- |
| Loan      | $1,000,000 |
| PD        | 4%         |
| LGD       | 50%        |
| EAD       | $1,000,000 |

### Expected Loss

```
EL = 0.04 × 0.50 × 1,000,000
EL = $20,000
```

The bank must maintain capital to absorb such losses.

If the bank maintains sufficient capital relative to its risk‑weighted assets, it satisfies **capital adequacy requirements**.

---

# Summary

Capital adequacy ensures banks remain safe and stable by maintaining enough capital to absorb losses from risky loans.

Key concepts include:

* Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)
* Basel regulations
* Probability of Default (PD)
* Loss Given Default (LGD)
* Exposure at Default (EAD)
* Expected Loss (EL)

These metrics together form the foundation of **modern credit risk management**.
